Emancipation and Freedom Monument

This monument stands in Lincoln Park, Washington DC. Dedicated in 1876, it was the first monument to Abraham Lincoln and the first major public monument in the United States funded entirely by formerly enslaved people.

The inscription reads: "Freedom's Memorial. In grateful memory of Abraham Lincoln. This monument was erected by the Western Sanitary Commission of Saint Louis, Mo.: with funds contributed solely by emancipated citizens of the United States declared free by his proclamation January 1st, A.D. 1863."

Historical Context

What makes this monument significant is not just what it depicts, but who paid for it. Charlotte Scott, a formerly enslaved woman from Virginia, donated the first $5 in 1865 - the day after Lincoln's assassination. Over 11 years, freedmen across the country contributed to complete the monument.

The depiction itself has been controversial since its unveiling. Frederick Douglass, speaking at the dedication, questioned whether it truly represented freedom or merely the transition from bondage. The formerly enslaved man is shown breaking chains but still kneeling, which many interpret as subservient rather than triumphant.

Why It Matters

This monument represents agency. It wasn't commissioned by the government or white philanthropists. It was funded entirely by people who had been enslaved, who chose to memorialize not just Lincoln but their own emancipation.

The controversy around its imagery doesn't diminish its historical significance - it enhances it. It shows how freedmen engaged with their own representation from the very beginning, how they claimed the right to tell their story even when that story was complicated.

This is evidence of continuity. From 1865 forward, Black Americans have been actively documenting, memorializing, and asserting their place in this nation's history.

About The Freedmen Star

This is a living archive of Black American heritage - documented as encountered, preserved as evidence, presented without apology.

We are the continuous people. Not diaspora. Not immigrants. The descendants of enslaved people who were here while the nation was being built, who remained through every era, whose lineage is unbroken.

What This Archive Documents

  • Monuments and memorials to our contributions and our people
  • Historical evidence of our presence and building from 1619 forward
  • Cultural expressions and innovations that shaped American identity
  • The continuous line from founding era to present day

What This Is Not

This is not focused on victimhood. This is focused on what we built, created, and contributed.

This is not about the African diaspora. This is specifically about American Descendants of Slavery and post-emancipation Freedmen - those whose lineage is tied to U.S. soil.

This is not political advocacy or reparations activism. This is documentation. Evidence. Memory work.

The Ideological Foundation

Like Indigenous peoples have their nations and histories, like Palestinians document their continuous presence, we document ours. Not claiming the state or its violence - but claiming our contributions, our culture, our unbreakable line.

We are not part of "African-American" as a hyphenated identity suggesting dual belonging. We are Americans whose ancestors were forced here, who built this nation's infrastructure and culture, who remained through every era. Our lineage is continuous where most others' is not.

This is our story. Our evidence. Our legacy.

— Ronnie, Curator
